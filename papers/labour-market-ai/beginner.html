Labour Market Matching Efficiency in the Age of AI

A plain-language version of Working Paper No. 001.

Perspective·2026·5 min read

What is the paper about?

This paper looks at the labour market for the professional services and the increasing adoption of AI within the recruitment process. It deploys a simple search and matching model, as developed from Diamond-Mortenson-Pissarides, and uses a simple mathematical toolkit (and a little bit of game theory) to determine its impact on equlibrium.

Why does it matter?

Unemployment and underemployment are costly for workers, firms and society. They are unable to consume as much as they would like, slowing the flow of funds around the economy and depressing growth. With unemployment firms are unable to fill vacancies, which reduces their productivity and output. Better matching can reduce unemployment, improve productivity, and help workers find roles that match their skillset.

Where AI fits in

The involvement of artificial intelligence in the hiring process is associated with its ability to serve as a intermediary for linking together potential employers and workers. Organizations employ applicant tracking systems and automated interviews to help sorting and ranking applicants, while job seekers are increasingly using artificial intelligence to write and customize their resumes. Thus, the "matching process" in question gets changed because it involves the reduction of some search costs and the increase in screening efficiency; however, it results in such drawbacks as an immense number of applicants and the dismissal of suitable candidates due to the absence of matching key words in their resumes. To put it simply, AI becomes involved in the hiring process at almost any stage from sourcing candidates to conducting interviews and making decisions about moving on with the applicants.

The catch

The same tools can also introduce new problems: biased screening, over-reliance on automated filters that reject good candidates for the wrong reasons, or an advantage for firms and workers who can afford the best AI tools while others fall behind. AI does not automatically make matching better, it changes the shape of the problem.

Main takeaway

However, while AI can make recruiting faster and more efficient due to better candidate search, automatic selection, and cheaper hiring, it may cause additional issues like inundation of employers with AI-driven applications or wrong elimination of potential candidates because of too strict selection algorithms. The main idea presented in the paper is that all these changes influence hiring results like vacancy numbers, wages, and unemployment but they do not automatically lead to the improvement of the labor market. What matters is whether the introduction of AI into recruiting shifts the power balance between employers and workers, which needs further investigation.
