Labour Market Matching Efficiency in the Age of AI

A deeper explanation of the same paper, still written to be readable.

Perspective·2026·9 min read

Matching efficiency and the Matching Function

Matching Function Matching Function is a central notion of search and matching theory, which helps to understand the transformation of unemployed individuals and vacant jobs into hired employees. Unlike the traditional literature, which implies an instantaneous equilibrium on the labour market, research takes into account the costliness and delay associated with searching and matching the proper candidate with the job opening because both employer and candidate have to look for the match. The matching function proposed by Peter Diamond, Dale Mortensen, and Christopher Pissarides contains such factors as the number of unemployed individuals, the number of vacancies, and matching efficiency, which shows how effective the labour market is in transforming job seekers and vacancies into hires. Matching efficiency can be high when suitable candidates and businesses match faster, thus leading to more successful hires with regard to the same amount of applicants and vacancies; matching efficiency can be low if a greater amount of effort and time is needed to make the same number of matches. According to the paper, there is the possibility for AI to influence matching efficiency since the use of AI technology takes place in recruiting both sides of the market; AI-powered applicant tracking systems and automated interviews may increase matching efficiency by selecting the right candidates but at the same time lower matching efficiency by congesting the application process and rejecting the right candidates due to strict screening process. Thus, changes in matching efficiency become one of the main areas through which AI impacts recruitment process.

How AI may change it

The role of AI in matching efficiency is linked to its impact on the way workers and employers search for and match with each other within the framework of the recruitment process. According to the conventional theory of search-and-matching, matching efficiency can be seen as an intrinsic feature of the labour market; however, this paper says that such an assumption is increasingly less applicable in light of the growing use of AI in the hiring process on the employer and worker sides. AI may positively affect matching efficiency by assisting recruiters in identifying appropriate applicants, analyzing their resumes using more sophisticated tools than keyword searches, and recommending candidates who match the skils required for the role. There are two major ways in which AI can lower the degree of matching efficiency. Firstly, the development of generative AI has resulted in an increased capacity for applicants to tailor their applications, causing a significant upsurge in application numbers and creating conditions of congestion in which employers find it hard to identify the most suitable candidates. Secondly, AI-based applicant tracking systems (ATS) can wrongly filter out qualified applicants based on keyword, credential, or employment history criteria, thus preventing productive matches between firms and protential hirers, denoted in the paper as being a Type II error borrowing the convention from statistics.

The potential downside

The paper has listed out potential negatives of AI application in hiring processes, almost all of which tend to lessen the efficiency of the matching process instead of enhancing it. For instance, one potential negative of generative AI in hiring is congestion of applications, which means that applicants are enabled by generative AI to submit numerous applications within a short period. The second potential problem identified by the paper is inaccurate screening due to ATS which tends to reject applicants based on their CVs, where the latter do not match the required criteria in terms of skills, keywords, or work experience, despite the applicants having the necessary capabilities to fulfill job requirements. The third negative is related to duration dependence, according to which automation of hiring processes would harm applicants who have gaps in their employment history since automatic systems would discriminate against them. In addition, one more potential negative of hiring by means of AI refers to task substitution that would be especially relevant for entry-level jobs of professionals and decrease the need for hiring human beings. Finally, hiring by means of AI might lead to workers' bargaining weakness. The hiring firms now faces a reduced cost for continuing the search for candidates by using automated system reducing the bargaining power of workers.

Why the adjustment period matters

The importance of the adjustment period lies in the fact that the impact of AI in recruitment will probably take some time to show itself rather than happening all at once since the labour market will require time to adjust to these impacts as workers, employers and recruitment technology react to one another. The author claims that AI transforms the job search process of the workers, the application screening process of the firms and even changes the value of various kinds of labour, particularly routine and entry-level work. As a result, during the adjustment period workers who have lost their jobs because of AI might find themselves unable to get a new one due to the fact that the automated recruitment process might not like employment breaks or might reject applicants because of their stringent screening standards. In addition, it may take some time for the companies to adapt their AI technology and discern the truly qualified candidates among the growing number of candidates applying with the help of the technology. Thus, as a consequence of such a mis-match, unemployment rises and matching efficiency drops until the market adjusts.

What would make the optimistic case more convincing

The strongest version of the optimistic case rests on AI reducing information asymmetry; helping workers see opportunities and helping firms see qualified candidates they would otherwise miss. That case gets stronger with transparent screening criteria, portable and interoperable data about skills and work history, and broad access to matching tools rather than access concentrated among a subset of firms or workers. Where those conditions are absent, the same technology is more likely to reproduce, or even harden, existing frictions rather than dissolve them.
