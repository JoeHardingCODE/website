Is Age a Problem in Modern Society?

Perspective · 2026 · 7 min read

The average American CEO retires at age sixty-two. Twenty percent of the members of congress are already past seventy. Chuck Grassley is ninety-two years old. None of this is to knock on anyone’s ability to do their job, democracy at its core supposedly elects those best suited for the role, but a gerontocracy is simply a curious way to structure a government.

A Geontocracy, by the Numbers

If the opening did not convey the scale. Here’s some more context. The median age in the United States sits in the high thirties, the average age in congress is roughly two decades older, with it being the third oldest senate in history. The median age of the senators is sixty-four, twenty-four sitting members of congress are eighty or older and more than half of these are running for re-election.

None of this happened by accident, though. It's less about personal decline and more about incumbency, about how safe seats remove any real pressure to retire on schedule, and how committee power in Congress still runs almost entirely on seniority. Stay put long enough and you get to chair something. A term-limits advocate made a point to NBC News that I keep turning over: he doesn't think longevity itself is the disease so much as a symptom of a system where incumbents rarely face real competition.

The wealth-generational-conflict version

Much of the political side is already saturated by the media. What I’m more interested in is the economic ramification. A legislature and an electorate skew this far from the countries median age will default time and time again to protecting existing wealth rather than the ambitious wealth creation that the west has long been craving. Baby Boomers (those born in the short aftermath of WWII) make up roughly 20% of the population but hold 51% of the country’s wealth, Millennials occupy a similar share of the demographic hold roughly 15% of the wealth. Boomers own 54 percent of all household stock, worth more than $25 trillion; millennials hold about 8 percent, worth under $4 trillion. Even pensions, the one asset class that isn't supposed to compound the way stocks do, split close to five to one in boomers' favour.

The mistake is treating a demographic trend and a political grievance as if they were the same argument. They overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

Housing and Debt

Housing follows a similar pattern. The median age for becoming a home-owner reach 40 in 2025. There are many reasons for this; mortgages rates, zoning boards, legislation and congress who benefit every time a restriction on new builds is passed (it pushes their own real estate portfolios up). Debt is the same, student debt drives most of the disproportioned indebtedness the younger years face, tuition has increased 300% since the 1980s a rate hardly explained by just ‘matching inflation’.

Registration and Turnout

Unfortunately, a lot of this comes down to registration and voting, not malice from the elderly. Fewer than 30 percent of 18-year-olds are registered to vote in a typical midterm, and even in a presidential year, registration for that age group tops out around 44 percent, against roughly 75 percent of Americans 45 and older. But young people who do get registered actually show up: registered voters ages 18 to 24 have turned out above 75 percent in every presidential election since 2004. The problem isn't that young people don't care enough to vote. It's that a much smaller share of them ever make it onto the rolls in the first place, and the people currently in office have little incentive to spend political capital fixing that. So that while the elections do not represent the entire country, they may represent the turnout demographic, much to the younger population’s detriment.

Age isn't the villain

Age is not the villain, and I wish not to push gerontophobia. Some of the longest-serving members of Congress do deliver real results for their districts, and competence/experience is worth something in a legislature. The term-limits argument probably has it right that the deeper problem is the absence of real competition, not old age on its own, and the same logic holds for turnout. Youth participation spiked well above the historical floor in 2018, 2020, and 2022 alike, which suggests a close, high-stakes race pulls young voters out at rates the tired "kids don't vote" story doesn't account for. The gap narrows when someone gives people an actual reason to show up, so it may simply be a ‘get the youth out to vote kind of problem’. It can not be that difficult, right?
