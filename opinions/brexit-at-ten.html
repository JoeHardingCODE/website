Brexit at Ten

Perspective · 2026 · 11 min read

Ten years ago this June, a bus told Britain that leaving the EU would free up £350 million a week for the NHS. Like many a bus this one never showed up. But the tenth anniversary of the referendum has brought a wave of fresh research on what Brexit actually did to trade, investment, prices, fishing, immigration, and the public finances.

The economy is smaller than it would have been

I'd like to start with the headline figure because most of what follows contributes to it. The Office for Budget Responsibility has assumed since 2020 that Brexit will eventually cut UK productivity by 4% compared with staying in the EU, phased in over about fifteen years. In its most recent reviews, the OBR says this forecast is "broadly on track": trade intensity has fallen roughly as predicted, and business investment has undershot expectations.

Other estimates run higher. Goldman Sachs updated its model to mark the anniversary and now puts the gap at around 6%, up from the 5% it estimated two years earlier. NIESR said current research spanning macro models and firm-level data puts the loss anywhere from 4% to 10%. Stanford economist Nicholas Bloom estimated 6–8% by 2025. These figures should be taken with a pinch of salt, isolating a cause with economic data is notoriously hard, meaning many figures are either inflated, or deflated, with noise and confounding factors. Considering what we know about the world post-Brexit, I would hazard a guess on these being inflated.

A 4% hit to an economy Britain's size crudely works out to somewhere around £125 billion a year and puts the associated tax loss at roughly £40 billion between 2019 and 2024 alone, a period in which the government raised taxes by £100 billion to help its own fiscal gap. It makes you think: If only there were £40 billion lying around then you'd have paid less in tax. The UK's growth trajectory has shifted too. In the twenty years before the referendum, it tracked the United States fairly closely; in the decade since, it looks a lot more like the eurozone's dampened growth.

Global Britain's trade deals were never going to be enough

One of the promises was that Britain would strike better deals alone than it ever could as part of the EU. It has struck deals, around 70 since 2020, according to trade groups, but the overwhelming majority are continuity agreements that simply carried over terms the UK already had as an EU member.

These new agreements are those with Australia and New Zealand, as well as joining the CPTPP economic bloc in the Pacific region. According to the UK government's own impact assessments, the long-term increase in GDP resulting from the Australia agreement is estimated at 0.08%, while the New Zealand agreement at 0.03%. Joining CPTPP adds between 0.06% and 0.23%, depending on which model is seen as the most reliable one. Considering all points prior, a long-term gain resulting from these deals is estimated to be around 1% of GDP according to the experts at the Centre for European Reform, in comparison to Brexit's costs that, in the vast majority of analyses, amount to four to eight times more.

In contrast to this, trade with the EU countries has decreased. The estimates of the deficit of UK goods exports vary within the range of 10% to 25%, and the UK suffered a fall of £74 billion in goods exports after Brexit. The exports of food products to the EU decreased by one fourth to a third. Some 20,000 small UK businesses have stopped selling their goods to the EU, while a Federation of Small Businesses survey conducted in May found that a third of European-exporting SMEs are considering ceasing trade to the bloc.

It isn't all bad news on trade. Services have held up far better than goods, Britain is now the third-fastest-growing services exporter in the G7, with finance, insurance and professional services all posting gains.

Fishing

No group backed Brexit more enthusiastically than Britain's fishing communities, one survey found 92% of the industry intended to vote Leave in 2016, drawn by promises that Britain would become an "independent coastal state" with full control of its own waters. It hasn't worked out that way. The December 2020 trade deal preserved most of the EU's existing access to UK waters, breaking a specific pledge to exclude EU boats from the six-to-twelve-nautical-mile zone where most small UK boats operate.

Things got worse for the industry last May, when the Starmer government agreed to extend EU fishing access to UK waters for twelve years, until 2038, as part of a wider reset of relations with Brussels.

The grocery bill

According to the research by the LSE's Centre for Economic Performance, new customs and regulatory inspections on food imports have cost an average British family an extra £250 on its weekly food bill between the end of 2019 and March 2023, which means a loss of some £7 billion in total, because the prices went up by 25 percent instead of the anticipated 17 percent due to these additional expenses. Thus, Brexit can be held responsible for one-third of Britain's food price inflation over that period.

According to more recent estimations made at the time of the anniversary of Brexit, UK retail food prices are estimated to rise by 50% during the five years since November 2016 until November 2026 – a rate that was achieved in twenty years prior to Brexit. Indeed, not everything can be attributed to Brexit. However, non-tariff barriers, particularly sanitary and veterinary checks, are still relevant.

Immigration: control?

The slogan "take back control of our borders" would imply to most Leave supporters that fewer immigrants come into the country. On the contrary, net immigration increased from an average level of about 250,000 per year during the 2010s to more than 900,000 in 2023, the highest level ever and nearly three to four times above pre-Brexit levels.

It is almost the complete opposite of expectations. As promised, freedom of movement with the EU was abolished, and net EU immigration is negative. However, the very same measure that ended freedom of movement led to introducing a liberal points-based visa system to the rest of the world, and the number of non-EU immigrants exploded to compensate for the difference and even surpass it, as care worker visas, student visas, and post-study work rights were all relaxed. The Centre for European Reform estimates that considering the impact of Brexit on Britain, the number of immigrants in the country in 2024 is higher than if the country had stayed in the EU.

Numbers have since fallen sharply, down to somewhere between 170,000 and 200,000 in 2025 as the government tightened salary thresholds, banned most student dependants and closed the overseas care worker route. Whether that's a stable new normal or a temporary correction after the spike isn't clear yet. What is clear is that "taking back control" ended up meaning the government could set its own rules, and for several years after 2021, it chose remarkably loose ones, remarkably similar to those of the EU.

The City of London

A case that's especially intriguing is that of financial services, where both sides — pre-Brexit fears about leaving Europe and post-Brexit boasts about doing well after leaving Europe — are revealed to be misguided. Since the referendum, more than 440 companies of the financial industry have moved part of their operations to the EU, bringing along about £900 billion of assets into the EU's territory, mainly into Paris, Frankfurt and Dublin. Recent research shows that there has been a structural change in the interrelationship between London and continental financial markets: whereas prior to Brexit any problems in the UK's financial sector would influence the whole of Europe, the opposite seems to happen now.

However, the City remains strong. The number of jobs in the Square Mile approaches the record levels of around 676,000 people employed, growing by more than a quarter since 2019. The banks' profit is growing and in 2026 even JP Morgan started to transfer some positions from Paris to London. As Michael Mainelli, the Lord Mayor of the City in 2023 and 2024 noted, Brexit undoubtedly had a weakening effect on the City's standing, however, the financial sectors of Europe are also struggling such that both London and Europe are losing out to their American and Asian counterparts.

Sovereignty, or something like it

Then, there is the pledge that is difficult to measure in numbers, that of sovereignty. On this score, things have been somewhat patchy. Indeed, the Parliament now possesses full sovereignty in terms of regulation making and setting trade policies without any need to coordinate with Brussels, which is a definite structural departure from EU membership. Since Brussels cannot wield sovereign powers anymore, the powers now pass to our Parliament. It is a logical chain of succession and is not necessarily a plus for Brexit unless the Parliament chooses a policy direction which is different from that pursued within the EU and reaps additional benefits as a result.

However, the "bonfire of EU red tape" which was promised in the context of the 2022 Conservative leadership contest turned out to be an illusion. As the government led by Rishi Sunak gave up on the plan to repeal all of the laws inherited from the EU automatically by the end of 2023 after officials advised against doing so, it ended up retaining seemingly 90% of these rules. The critics from both the right and the left also noted that most of the "sovereignty taken back" has ended up going to ministers issuing statutory instruments with minimal parliamentary scrutiny, rather than to backbench MPs, which is not quite the return of power to Parliament that the campaign implied.

This issue I find with this is that Brussel was never a 'Big Brother' that the Brexiters had made it out to be. I think a simple logic can undermine this entire sovereignty argument, if the EU subject their own citizens to such laws, then how bad can these laws really be. And if the UK had no sovereignty or say at all within the European parliament then what was Farge doing for 20 years?

What the public thinks now

A decade on, the public verdict is fairly stark. YouGov's tracker has shown a majority saying Brexit was the wrong decision consistently since mid-2022, and its most recent poll around the anniversary put it at 57% wrong to 30% right meaning roughly six in ten Britons call Brexit a failure outright. The Conservative Party and Boris Johnson top the list of who gets the blame for that, well ahead of the EU itself or Nigel Farage.

As with many things in this country, it isn't a uniform picture. About two-thirds of people who voted Leave in 2016 say they'd do it again, why? Farage himself told the BBC around the anniversary that Brexit was "absolutely the right thing to do". And despite all the regret, there's no great rush to reverse course: recent Ipsos polling finds only about 1% of Britons volunteer Europe or Brexit as the most important issue facing the country, rising to 4% when people can name more than one, and only 39% of the public think rejoining the EU should be a government priority even in principle.

It makes me wonder though that if those polled understood that Brexit could be a long run cause of the problems first on their list, e.g. cost of living or immigration, whether their ordinal ranking would remain the same.

The generational split is the sharpest divide of all, with younger voters far more likely to want a return to the EU than older ones, a gap that will only widen as the electorate turns over.

The other side of the ledger

None of this is the whole story, and it's worth presenting some sort of counterargument. The biggest complication is timing. Britain left the single market weeks before COVID-19 shut down the global economy, and less than two years before Russia's invasion of Ukraine triggered an energy crisis that hit European economies hard. Separating Brexit's specific contribution from these overlapping shocks is difficult, and economists across the spectrum acknowledge it. Even Goldman Sachs, whose analysts estimate a 6% GDP hit, build in what they call robustness checks precisely because other factors could be inflating that figure; their most conservative reading still finds at least 4%, but they're careful not to claim false precision.

There are real, if modest, wins too. Britain's accession to CPTPP, and it's from-scratch deals with Australia and New Zealand, didn't exist before Brexit, whatever their limited economic size (both New Zealand and Australia have trade agreements with the EU). Services exports have proven far more resilient than goods. Northern Ireland, which retains dual access to both the UK and EU markets under the Brexit deal's special arrangements, has actually outgrown the rest of the UK, with its economy expanding 16.5% between 2015 and 2023, a point Brexit supporters reasonably raise as evidence that market access, not sovereignty as such, was always the real economic driver. Analysts at the Institute of Economic Affairs, argue the OBR's 4% figure rests on shaky assumptions about trade intensity that haven't held up well against the actual post-Brexit trade data.

One commonly cited win doesn't survive scrutiny, though: the claim that Brexit sped up Britain's Covid vaccine rollout. Fact-checkers at Full Fact, the Institute for Government and others have repeatedly found that the UK's early vaccine approval relied on an EU regulation letting member states authorize medicines in a health emergency, a provision Britain used while still inside the bloc's legal orbit during the transition period. The UK's advantage came from a well-run procurement operation, not from having left the EU.

Finally, there's an argument that isn't really about economics at all. For a meaningful share of Leave voters, control over immigration policy and lawmaking was worth an economic cost, whatever that cost turned out to be. That's a values trade-off, not a factual claim, and it's not one that GDP figures can settle either way. For what it's worth, Goldman Sachs thinks the worst of the "Brexit headwind" may now be behind Britain, and forecasts a pickup in growth over the coming years, particularly if the current push for closer UK-EU cooperation continues.

Ten years on

Add it up, and the pattern holds fairly consistently across every specific, checkable promise: the £350 million for the NHS, the trade deals that would outweigh single market membership, the fishing revival, the lower immigration numbers. Measured against what actually happened, each one falls short. The one promise that was delivered in a formal sense, sovereignty over laws and borders, turned out to be messier in practice than the slogan suggested, and its economic value is disputed even by people who think it was worth having.

What happens next is unclear. Public opinion has soured on the decision without settling on an alternative, the government is pursuing closer trade and security cooperation with Brussels while ruling out single market or customs union membership, and Reform UK is gaining ground on the argument that Brexit simply wasn't done properly. Ten years in, Britain is still working out what leaving the EU actually meant. On the numbers so far, the honest answer is that it cost more than it delivered. Although as you might guess I am an ardent remainer, I'll get over it I'm sure.
